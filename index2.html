We Get Tết

For us to be experts in Tết, we had to go back to the start and understand how Tết has changed over the years. We commissioned extensive research and we’d like to share our findings with you.


  • Tet - The Origins

    As you know, Tết is Vietnamese Lunar New Year and the most important festival in Vietnam. The word is a shortened form of Tết Nguyên Đán, meaning ‘Feast of the First Morning of the First Day’. The occasion celebrates the arrival of Spring. It’s a time for families to get together and celebrate the beginning of the New Year with renewed hope. While Lunar New Year celebrations originated in China and were brought to many East Asian countries, Vietnam is the only country that insists on calling it by another name, Tết, and in doing so have also made it their own.

    Tết has traditionally had many key rituals and practices – some tracing their roots back to the festival’s Chinese origins, some being uniquely Vietnamese. For a nation of farmers attached to the land for thousands of years, it has always been a festival marking the communion of man with nature. In the flow of seasons, it is a pause during which both the field and farmer enjoy some rest after twelve months of hard work. And to this day it is a period of renewal and it’s this idea that defines the customs of Tết.

  • Tet – The Rituals

    Families believe that their activities during Tết should involve happiness, joy and good luck. That's why even before the beginning of Tết, it's customary for families to make preparations. Cleaning and painting their home in anticipation of Spring, settling old debts or disputes and pledge to behave nicely and work hard in the New Year.

    Traditionally people also buy new clothes to celebrate the occasion. Paying homage to ancestors is another important part of Tết with families praying in Temples, Churches and Pagodas. They visit the graves of their relatives and make altars in their homes. These have photographs of their ancestors and they offer them symbolic gifts in the form of food, flowers and incense. The night before the New Year, families perform a ritual where incense sticks are burned to invite the spirits of their ancestors to join them in celebration. This is also a time to bid farewell to the family's Kitchen God (Ong Tao), who then returns to heaven to report on their behavior in the past year to the Jade Emperor. They leave cookies out for Ong Tao to get a favorable report and release goldfish in a lake to transport him to heaven.

    During Tết, houses are decorated with several different things. Cay Neu is a small bamboo tree planted in the front of the house. Hoa Mai is a yellow blossom that represents Spring. Red banners are hung on front doors as it's believed the colour prevents evil spirits from entering the house. Adults give fancy red envelopes to children full of Li Xi or ‘lucky money’, always in even denominations since odd numbers are considered bad luck.

    When it's time for Le Tru Tich, the official start of Tết, people fill the streets in celebration of the New Year. They try to make as much noise as possible using anything from firecrackers, drums, bells and gongs, to simple wooden instruments to ward off evil spirits. Xong Dat is another important custom for the first day of Tết to ensure good luck for the coming year. It's critical that the first person who visits a family's house in the New Year is someone who has enjoyed good luck during the previous year. The belief is that his or her karma can also influence the family's fortune in the coming year.

  • Tet – The Evolution

    Over the years, some of these rituals have stopped or changed due to practical, economic or environmental circumstances. Now firecrackers are banned and there is only the controlled Government fireworks display in HCMC and Hanoi. While families got together to make Banh Chung in the past, they often buy these from shops now. Almost 30% of the Vietnamese surveyed by FTA in 2013 said that they bought everything for Tết from stores. 21% said the traditional preparation activities have all but disappeared. But it’s not just changes at a family level. Stores themselves have changed their tradition of being shut for the entire period to just the first day of Tết. Only one day now then business as usual.

    These changes have started having an impact on the ‘atmosphere’ of Tết. When we spoke to teens, young adults and older generations, they all told us that they feel Tết isn’t what it used to be. It has lost its spirit – the magic of the family gathering together , preparing together. The heart-warming feeling of everyone being busy and active with Tết rituals. Even the stores being shut made Tết holidays unique and special. 32.5% of Vietnamese surveyed said Tết no longer had the warm, happy atmosphere as it used to. “When the stores stayed shut for 2 weeks, we had to prepare enough food for the whole time, shopping in advance for everything we’d need. But now they are open after the first day, there is nothing we need to do. It just doesn’t feel the same anymore” – Ngoc, 27 years, HCMC

    What’s more, with more families from cities living abroad and more migration from the rural parts to the cities (and the migrants going back to their home towns), Tết in the cities like HCMC, Hanoi and Danang is much quieter. In some ways, it has lost its energy and vibe. And today, many families are even choosing to use the Tết break as a time to go away on holiday to foreign locations.

    “When we were young, when Tết came, the streets would be crowded and bustling – everyone out celebrating together or doing something. Now the streets are so empty, we can lie down on the streets. It’s not as fun as it used to be” – Hanh, 21 years, HCMC

    In addition to this, Tết has started posing an economic strain on Vietnamese – with many families even having to take loans out for the festivities along with the cost of going back to hometowns increasing dramatically year on year. There is also a significant financial benefit working through Tết instead of taking off to be home with family. These economic considerations have become such a significant problem that 1 in 4 Vietnamese avoid celebrating Tết.

  • Tet – The Importance

    But even as these sentiments change, Tết still remains a very dear festival to Vietnamese, young and old alike. When asked if Tết and the Western New Year should be merged or if Tết should disappear, Vietnamese were strongly against it. These proposals led a controversial debate earlier this year when it was suggested by prominent economists. There is a deep-rooted sense that ‘this is ours’, ‘it is a part of our culture and our tradition’, ‘if we lose Tết, we will lose a part of who we are’. These were the comments we heard when challenging Vietnamese teens and young adults, especially heartening when they seem to be the ones who are most ‘bored’ during Tết. This is what makes Tết a crucial celebration for the Vietnamese even today and for marketers the most important season to drive sales volumes. It is therefore critical to understand the changing nature of Tết and what it means so as to effectively engage the Vietnamese during this important festival. And to play a role in their lives to make Tết something to cherish again.

    Brands that do well during Tết are the ones that understand these dilemmas and help resolve them by making Tết more relevant to today’s cultural context. When we look at 4 of the top 5 brands in Tết 2015, we see that these are brands that have done exactly that. Coca Cola, the number 1 brand for the fourth year running, reminded Vietnamese about the true meaning of Tết – helping the youth reengage with family and understand the importance of time over money. OMO also tapped in to the economic constraints imposed by Tết on family reunions. The brand told the story of the young girl who helps the old vendor make enough money to afford the increasingly expensive travel costs to return home. Neptune tapped into the growing behavior of young families using Tết as a holiday to go away to travel, remindeding them of the need to go back home to their older generations who were longing to see them. Vinacafe interestingly moved away from traditional Tết themes while still focusing on key Tết construct of connecting families. They showed that they understood the difficulty by helping youths express themselves to their parents.

2012

Tet has always been a time for families to get together. But we found that with time, especially for the youth and young adults, this notion of family had taken on a much more extended meaning to include friends, colleagues, neighbors, etc. We tapped in to the insight of the redefined and extended notion of ‘family’ and Coke acted as an enabler for connecting with larger personal groups and then bringing the family together.

2013

Once again leveraging on the most critical aspect of Tet – family togetherness. We realized that while in the past everyone took time off to celebrate Tet with their family, with the increasing economic demands of today’s Vietnam, many people in the lower and middle income brackets chose to work through Tet to earn extra money and help provide for their families. This however came in the way of families celebrating Tet together. We tapped in to the insight of blue collar workers needing to work through Tet to make additional income and Coke brought the family together by taking Tet to the father who was working as a bus driver through Tet.

2014

We found that the old Tet traditions had lost its relevance amongst the youth, making them feel disengaged and as a fall out, disconnected from their family and not spending time together. We tapped in to the insight that the youth feel bored and disconnected during Tet and Coke helped the youth reengage by bringing their own twist to Tet chores and helping family reconnect and bond over doing the “refreshed” rituals together.

2015

We found that 1 in 4 young adults were choosing not to go back home to celebrate Tet with their families because of the growing economic burden on them in terms of having to buy expensive gifts to take back home and expenses on Tet celebrations once they were there. We tapped in to the insight on the growing economic pressure for young adults and Coke reminded families that it is time with family that is more important than the money you spend on them.

Results:

Our stories resonated well with youth, young adults and their families resulting in Coca Cola being the #1 brand of Tet for 4 years consecutively according to Millward Brown’s post Tet survey. What’s more, we were not just loved but were also effective in driving sales year on year with a significant volume growth to show for it.
