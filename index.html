  • Tet - The Origins

    Tet is the Vietnamese Lunar New Year and the most important festival in Vietnam. The word is a shortened form of Tết Nguyên Đán meaning "Feast of the First Morning of the First Day" and the occasion celebrates the arrival of spring. It is a time for families to get together and celebrate the beginning of a new year with renewed hope and vigor. While Lunar New Year celebrations originated in China and was brought to many East Asian countries, Vietnam is the only country that insists on calling it by another name “Tet” and in doing so have also made it their own.

    Tet has traditionally had many key rituals and practices – some tracing their roots back to the festival’s Chinese origins, some being uniquely Vietnamese. For a nation of farmers attached to the land for millennia, it has always been a festival marking the communion of man with nature. In the flow of seasons it is a pause during which both the field and the tiller enjoy some rest after twelve months of labor. And to this day it is a period of universal renewal and it is this notion of renewal that defines the customs of Tet.

  • Tet – The Rituals

    Over the years some of these rituals have stopped or changed due to practical economic or environmental considerations. Now firecrackers are banned and there is only the display by the government in HCMC and Hanoi. While families got together to make banh chung in the past, they now buy this and other preparations from stores. 29.9% of the Vietnamese surveyed by FTA in 2013 said that they bought Tet preparation at the store and 21% said the traditional preparation activities are almost gone. But it’s not just changes at a family level; stores themselves have changed their tradition of being shut for the entire period of Tet to just the first day of Tet.

    These changes have started having an impact on the ‘atmosphere’ of Tet itself. When we spoke to teens, young adults and even the older generations, they all universally feel that Tet isn’t what it used to be. It’s lost it’s spirit – a spirit that was engendered by the communal family gathering together to make preparations together, the magical feeling of everyone being busy and active with the celebrations of Tet rituals and even the stores being shut made the Tet holidays unique and special. 32.5% of Vietnamese surveyed by FTA in 2013 said Tet no longer had the warm and happy atmosphere as it did before.

    “When the stores stayed shut for 2 weeks, we had to prepare enough food for the whole time, shop in advance for everything we would need. But now they are open after the first day, there is nothing we need to do. It just doesn’t feel the same anymore”
    – Ngoc, 27 years, HCMC

    What’s more, with more families from the cities living abroad and more migration from the rural parts to the cities (and the migrants going back to their home towns), Tet in the cities like HCMC, Hanoi and Danang is much quieter and in some ways has lost it’s energy and vibe. And today, many families are even choosing to use the Tet break as a time to go away on holiday to foreign locations. “ When we were young, when Tet came, the streets would be crowded and bustling - everyone out celebrating together or doing something. Now the streets are so empty, we can lie down on the streets. It’s not as fun as it used to be”
    – Hanh, 21 years, HCMC

    And additional to all this, Tet has started posing an economic strain on Vietnamese – with many families even having to take loans out for the festivities and the cost of going back to home towns increasing year on year. There is also a significant economic benefit from working through Tet instead of taking off to be at home with family. These economic considerations have become such a significant problem that 1 in 4 Vietnamese avoid celebrating Tet as they did before.

  • Tet – The Evolution

    But even as these sentiments change, Tet still remains a very dear festival to the Vietnamese young and old alike. When asked if Tet and the western New Year were merged or if there would be no Tet anymore, Vietnamese were strongly against it – even leading to a controversial debate earlier this year when prominent economists suggested this. There is a deep-rooted sense of “this is ours”, “it is a part of our culture and tradition”, “if we lose Tet, we will lose a part of who we are” when we spoke to Vietnamese teens and young adults about this – and it was especially heartening when they seem to be the one who are most ‘bored’ during Tet.

    This is what makes Tet the most important celebration for the Vietnamese even today and for marketers the most important season to drive sales volumes. It is therefore critical to understand the changing nuances of Tet and what it means so as to effectively engage with Vietnamese during this important festival and play a role in their lives and in making Tet something to cherish again.

    Brands that do well during Tet are the ones that understand these dilemmas and help resolve them by making Tet more relevant to today’s cultural context. When we look at 4 of the top 5 brands in Tet 2015, we see that these are brands that have done exactly that. Coca Cola the #1 brand this Tet reminded Vietnamese about the true meaning of Tet – helping the youth reengage with family and understand the importance of time over money. Omo tapped in to the economic constraints imposed by Tet on family reunions by telling the story of the young girl who helps the old vendor make enough money to afford the increasingly expensive ticket costs to go back home. Neptune tapped in to the growing behavior of young families using Tet as a holiday to go away to travel and reminded them of the need to go back home to their older generations who were longing to see them. Vinacafe interestingly moved away from traditional Tet themes while still focusing on key Tet construct of connecting families by understanding their difficulty helping youth express themselves to their parents.

    And year after year Phibious has proven to crack the Tet code to make Coke the #1 brand for 4 years consecutively. In 2012, we tapped in to the insight of the redefined and extended notion of ‘family’ and Coke acted as an enabler for connecting with larger personal groups and then bringing the family together. In 2013, we tapped in to the insight of blue collar workers needing to work through Tet to make additional income and Coke brought the family together by taking Tet to the father who was working as a bus driver through Tet. In 2014, we tapped in to the insight that the youth feel bored and disconnected during Tet and Coke helped the youth reengage by bringing their own twist to Tet chores and helping family reconnect. And finally in 2015, we tapped in to the insight on the growing economic pressure for young adults with the expectations of expensive gifts and Coke reminded families that it is time with family that is more important than the money you spend on them.

    With our engagements for Coke during Tet we realized the critical importance of understanding the changing cultural nuances of Tet. But that’s not the whole story. We also realized that it was critical to engage the right protagonist for the brand, that we needed to understand the impact of changing means of engagement with media of our target and thus push for the most relevant story on the most relevant touch points. In 2015, Coke’s brand preference increased significantly (due to confidentiality, the actual numbers cannot be revealed). And since our TVC remained the same as the previous year, this increase can be unilaterally associated with our digital film. We encouraged young Vietnamese to not be hindered by the idea of buying expensive gifts and go back home for Tet because what really mattered was time with family. The digital format allowed for more detailed and emotional story telling which clearly resonated with audiences becoming the top watched and shared video within 24 hours (beating super bowl ads) and over 6.5mn views in a 4 weeks.
