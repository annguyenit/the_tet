  • Tet - The Origins

    Tet is the Vietnamese Lunar New Year and the most important festival in Vietnam. The word is a shortened form of Tết Nguyên Đán meaning "Feast of the First Morning of the First Day" and the occasion celebrates the arrival of spring. It is a time for families to get together and celebrate the beginning of a new year with renewed hope and vigor. While Lunar New Year celebrations originated in China and was brought to many East Asian countries, Vietnam is the only country that insists on calling it by another name “Tet” and in doing so have also made it their own. Tet has traditionally had many key rituals and practices – some tracing their roots back to the festival’s Chinese origins, some being uniquely Vietnamese. For a nation of farmers attached to the land for millennia, it has always been a festival marking the communion of man with nature. In the flow of seasons it is a pause during which both the field and the tiller enjoy some rest after twelve months of labor. And to this day it is a period of universal renewal and it is this notion of renewal that defines the customs of Tet.

  • Tet – The Rituals

    Families believe that their activities during Têt must involve happiness, joy, and good luck. That's why even before the beginning of Têt, it's customary for families to prepare by cleaning and even painting their home in anticipation of spring, settle old debts and disputes, and pledge to behave nicely and work hard in the new year. People also traditionally buy new clothes to usher in the New Year. Paying homage to one's ancestors is another important component of Têt as families pray at temples, churches, and pagodas. They will visit the gravesites of their deceased family and construct altars in their homes containing photographs of their ancestors, offering them symbolic gifts in the form of food, flowers, and incense. The night before the New Year, families perform a ritual where incense sticks are burned, inviting the spirits of their ancestors to join them in celebration. This is also a time to bid farewell to the family's Kitchen God (Ong Tao), who then returns to heaven to report on the family's behavior in the past year to the Jade Emperor. They leave cookies out for Ong Tao to get a favorable report and also release goldfish in the lake to transport him to heaven.

  • Tet – The Evolution

    Over the years some of these rituals have stopped or changed due to practical economic or environmental considerations. Now firecrackers are banned and there is only the display by the government in HCMC and Hanoi. While families got together to make banh chung in the past, they now buy this and other preparations from stores. 29.9% of the Vietnamese surveyed by FTA in 2013 said that they bought Tet preparation at the store and 21% said the traditional preparation activities are almost gone. But it’s not just changes at a family level; stores themselves have changed their tradition of being shut for the entire period of Tet to just the first day of Tet.

  • Tet – The Importance

    But even as these sentiments change, Tet still remains a very dear festival to the Vietnamese young and old alike. When asked if Tet and the western New Year were merged or if there would be no Tet anymore, Vietnamese were strongly against it – even leading to a controversial debate earlier this year when prominent economists suggested this. There is a deep-rooted sense of “this is ours”, “it is a part of our culture and tradition”, “if we lose Tet, we will lose a part of who we are” when we spoke to Vietnamese teens and young adults about this – and it was especially heartening when they seem to be the one who are most ‘bored’ during Tet. This is what makes Tet the most important celebration for the Vietnamese even today and for marketers the most important season to drive sales volumes. It is therefore critical to understand the changing nuances of Tet and what it means so as to effectively engage with Vietnamese during this important festival and play a role in their lives and in making Tet something to cherish again.
